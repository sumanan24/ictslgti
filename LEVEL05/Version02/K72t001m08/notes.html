1. Introduction to Text Manipulation

1 Font, Size, Formats and Special Effects

Learning focus: What text manipulation means and how we change how text looks (font, size, format, effects, alignment).

What is Text Manipulation?

Text manipulation means changing how text looks and behaves: the type of letters (font), how big they are (size), bold or italic (format), shadows or colours (effects), and how lines line up (alignment).

In simple words: When you type a word, you can make it bigger, bolder, in a different style, or move it to the centre. All of that is text manipulation.

Font (Typeface)

A font is the style of the letters. For example: Arial looks clean and simple; Times New Roman looks formal; Comic Sans looks casual. Choosing the right font helps your design say the right thing.

Different fonts give different feelings. Use one or two fonts per design to keep it clear.

Size

Size is how big the text is. We often measure it in points (pt) or pixels (px). Bigger text gets more attention; smaller text is for details. Headings are usually larger than body text.

Formats (Bold, Italic, Underline)

  • Bold – text is thicker and stands out.
  • Italic – text slants; often used for emphasis or titles.
  • Underline – a line under the text (use carefully; too much looks messy).

Special Effects

Special effects include: shadow (text appears to float), outline (border around letters), colour, and gradient (colour that changes from one to another). Use effects to make important text stand out, but do not overuse them.

Alignment

Alignment is how lines of text line up:

  • Left – text starts from the left (normal for paragraphs).
  • Centre – text is in the middle (good for titles).
  • Right – text ends at the right.
  • Justify – both left and right edges are straight (like in newspapers).

Left, centre and right alignment

Text Manipulation Software

Software used for text includes: Microsoft Word, Google Docs, LibreOffice Writer, and inside design tools like Adobe InDesign or Canva. In graphic design we often add and edit text inside the design software.

2. Introduction to Graphic Designing

2 Importance of Graphic Designing

Learning focus: Why graphic design matters in business, branding and communication.

Graphic design is the art of arranging images, text and shapes to communicate a message. Good design helps people understand your message quickly and remember your brand.

In simple words: When you see a poster, logo or website, someone planned the colours, pictures and text. That planning is graphic design. It makes things look professional and easy to read.

  • Branding – logos and colours help people recognise a company.
  • Advertising – posters and banners attract attention.
  • Information – charts and layouts make data easy to understand.

Good graphic design makes messages clear and attractive.

3 Sketching, Prototyping and Design Rules

Learning focus: Sketching and prototyping before using software; Golden ratio and Gestalt theory.

Sketching and Prototyping

Before using the computer, many designers sketch ideas on paper. A prototype is a simple version of the design to test layout and ideas. Sketching is fast and helps you try many ideas without wasting time on the computer.

Golden Ratio

The golden ratio (about 1.618 : 1) is a proportion that appears in nature and art. Using it in layout (e.g. dividing a page or image in this ratio) often makes the design feel balanced and pleasing.

A rectangle divided by the golden ratio

Gestalt Theory

Gestalt ideas say that we see whole shapes and patterns, not just dots and lines. Important principles:

  • Proximity – things close together are seen as a group.
  • Similarity – similar colour or shape = same group.
  • Closure – we complete incomplete shapes in our mind.
  • Continuity – we follow lines and curves naturally.

Using these helps organise your design so the viewer understands it quickly.

Hardware and Software Requirements

For graphic design you typically need: a computer (PC or Mac), enough RAM (e.g. 8 GB or more), a good monitor (clear and colour-accurate if possible), and a mouse or graphics tablet. Software examples: Adobe Illustrator, CorelDRAW, Inkscape (free), Canva (online).

Layers

Layers are like transparent sheets stacked on top of each other. You put different parts of your design on different layers (e.g. background on one, text on another, logo on another). You can hide, show, reorder or edit one layer without affecting the others.

3. Scanning

5 Flatbed, Drum Scanners and OCR

Learning focus: How we turn paper documents and photos into digital images; flatbed vs drum scanners; OCR for text.

What is Scanning?

Scanning means using a device (scanner) to capture a paper document or photo and save it as a digital image file on the computer.

Flatbed Scanner

A flatbed scanner has a flat glass plate. You place the document face down on the glass, close the lid, and the scanner moves a light and sensor under the glass to capture the image. It is common in offices and homes. Good for single sheets, photos and books (if you can open them flat).

Flatbed scanner: document on glass, lid closed.

Drum Scanner

A drum scanner is used mainly in professional printing. The document is wrapped around a rotating drum and a light beam scans it. Drum scanners give very high quality and are used for high-end print work. They are expensive and not common in small offices.

OCR (Optical Character Recognition)

OCR is software that looks at a scanned image of text and converts it into editable text (e.g. in Word). So you can scan a printed page and then search or edit the words instead of just having a picture of the page.

Use OCR when you need to edit or reuse the text from a printed document. Without OCR you only get an image; with OCR you get actual text you can change.
Activity

If you have access to a flatbed scanner, scan one page. If your software has OCR, run it and open the result in a word processor to see the editable text.

4. Page Layout

6 Page Layout for Different Print Media

Learning focus: What page layout is and how it changes for different print products (brochure, poster, flyer, etc.).

Page layout is how you arrange text, images and graphics on a page: margins, columns, headings, and where pictures go. Different print media need different sizes and layouts.

  • Poster – usually large (e.g. A2, A1), one main message, big text and one strong image.
  • Flyer – one or two pages, simple layout, clear call-to-action.
  • Brochure – folded (e.g. 3-fold), sections for different topics, mix of text and images.
  • Business card – small fixed size, name, contact, maybe logo.
  • Magazine / booklet – multiple pages, columns, consistent margins and headings.

Always set the correct page size and margins at the start so the design fits the final print size.

Different print media need different page layouts.

5. Introduction to Image Editing

7 Importance, Camera Shots and Image Editing Standards

Learning focus: Why we edit images; basic camera shots and framing; scaling and image quality.

Why Image Editing?

We edit images to: improve brightness and colour, remove mistakes, crop to focus on the subject, resize for print or web, and combine several images. Good editing makes photos clearer and more professional.

Camera Shots and Framing

Even if you did not take the photo, understanding shots helps you crop and compose:

  • Close-up – face or small detail; shows emotion or detail.
  • Medium shot – from waist up; good for people talking.
  • Wide shot – full scene; shows place or context.
  • Framing – what you include or leave out; rule of thirds (placing important parts on one-third lines) often improves composition.

Close-up, medium and wide shot.

Scaling and Image Quality

Scaling means making an image larger or smaller. If you make a small image much bigger, it can look blurry (pixelated) because the software is guessing extra pixels. For print we usually need enough resolution (e.g. 300 DPI for good quality). Image quality is affected by resolution, sharpness and compression. Avoid enlarging low-resolution images too much.

Rule of thumb: for print at 300 DPI, an A4 page needs about 2480 × 3508 pixels. For screen, 72–150 DPI is often enough.

8 Adobe Photoshop 2026 & Illustrator 2026: All Tools and Panels

Learning focus: Complete guide to the tools and panels in Adobe Photoshop 2026 (image editing) and Adobe Illustrator 2026 (vector graphics). Each tool and each main tab/panel is explained so you can work confidently at beginner level.

Adobe Photoshop 2026 – Workspace Overview

When you open Photoshop, the workspace has these parts:

  • Menu bar (top) – File, Edit, Image, Layer, Type, Select, Filter, 3D, View, Window, Help. Each menu holds commands for that topic.
  • Options bar (below menu bar) – Shows settings for the tool you have selected (e.g. brush size, opacity, tolerance). It changes when you switch tools.
  • Tools panel (left) – A vertical strip of tool icons. Click a tool to use it; many icons have a small arrow: click and hold to see hidden tools in that group.
  • Document window (centre) – Your image (canvas). You edit here.
  • Panels (right, or docked) – Tabs like Layers, Color, Properties, Channels, Paths. Each panel has a specific job; you can show/hide them from the Window menu.

Photoshop 2026 workspace: menu bar, Options bar, Tools panel (left), document (centre), panels (right).

Photoshop 2026 – Tools Panel (All Tools Explained)

The Tools panel is grouped by function. Below is what each tool does. Tools with a small triangle have more tools under them (click and hold to see them).

1. Move and Selection Tools

  • Move (V) – Moves the selected layer or selection. Use it to drag content around the canvas.
  • Rectangular Marquee (M) – Draws a rectangular selection. Hold Shift for a square.
  • Elliptical Marquee (M) – Draws an oval/circle selection. Good for round objects.
  • Lasso (L) – Draw a freehand selection by hand. Use for irregular shapes.
  • Polygonal Lasso (L) – Clicks to add straight segments; good for angles and straight edges.
  • Magnetic Lasso (L) – Snaps to edges as you move; useful when the object has clear borders.
  • Object Selection – Draw a rough box; Photoshop tries to select the main object inside.
  • Quick Selection (W) – Brush over an area; it selects similar colours and edges. Good for fast selections.
  • Magic Wand (W) – Click a colour; it selects all similar-coloured pixels. Use Tolerance in the Options bar to control how “similar” is.
  • Selection Brush – Paint to add or subtract from a selection like painting a mask.
Selection means “the area that will be affected.” Whatever you do (paint, delete, filter) only affects the selected area. No selection often means “the whole layer.”

2. Crop and Slice Tools

  • Crop (C) – Trims the image to a rectangle. You drag the corners; press Enter to apply. Used to change composition or remove edges.
  • Perspective Crop – Crop and correct perspective (e.g. a photo of a building taken at an angle).
  • Slice (C) – Cuts the image into sections, often for web (each slice can be saved as a separate image).

3. Measurement Tools

  • Eyedropper (I) – Click anywhere on the image to set the foreground colour to that pixel’s colour. Essential for matching colours.
  • Color Sampler (I) – Places markers that show colour values (e.g. RGB) at specific points. Used for checking colour consistency.
  • Ruler (I) – Draw a line to measure distance and angle. Info appears in the Options bar.
  • Note (I) – Add a text note stuck to the image (for collaboration or reminders).
  • Count (I) – Click to place numbered markers. Used for counting objects in an image.

4. Retouching and Painting Tools

  • Spot Healing Brush (J) – Click or brush over a small flaw (spot, blemish); Photoshop blends it with surrounding pixels. No need to set a source.
  • Healing Brush (J) – You Alt+click to set a “source” area, then paint over the flaw. Good for copying texture from one place to fix another.
  • Patch (J) – Draw a selection around the area to fix, then drag it to a good area; Photoshop blends it.
  • Red Eye (J) – Click on red eyes in a photo to remove the red colour.
  • Brush (B) – Paints with the foreground colour. Size, hardness, opacity and flow are in the Options bar. The main painting tool.
  • Pencil (B) – Like Brush but with hard edges (no softness).
  • Color Replacement (B) – Paints to change one colour to another while keeping shading.
  • Mixer Brush (B) – Simulates mixing paint; can blend colours on the canvas.
  • Clone Stamp (S) – Alt+click to set a source, then paint to copy pixels from that source. Used to duplicate or cover areas.
  • Pattern Stamp (S) – Paints using a pattern instead of copying from the image.
  • Eraser (E) – Removes pixels (or reveals the layer below). Soft or hard edge like a brush.
  • Background Eraser (E) – Erases similar colours; often used to remove a background.
  • Magic Eraser (E) – Click to erase all similar-coloured pixels (like Magic Wand + delete).
  • Gradient (G) – Fills with a gradient (one colour fading to another). Drag to set direction and length.
  • Paint Bucket (G) – Fills a contiguous area of similar colour with the foreground colour or a pattern.

5. Drawing and Type Tools

  • Pen (P) – Draws paths using anchor points and curves. Paths can be turned into selections or strokes. Professional way to get smooth shapes.
  • Freeform Pen (P) – Draws a path by hand; Photoshop adds anchor points automatically.
  • Horizontal Type (T) – Click to type text horizontally. You get a text layer; font, size and colour are in the Options bar.
  • Vertical Type (T) – Types text in a vertical line.
  • Rectangle (U) – Draws a rectangle shape (vector shape layer). Same key cycles: Rounded Rectangle, Ellipse, Polygon, Line, Custom Shape.
  • Path Selection (A) – Selects and moves a whole path.
  • Direct Selection (A) – Selects and moves individual anchor points on a path to reshape it.

6. Navigation Tools

  • Hand (H) – Drag to pan around the canvas without zooming. Double-click the Hand icon to fit the image to the screen.
  • Rotate View (R) – Rotates the view of the canvas (for drawing at an angle); does not rotate the actual image.
  • Zoom (Z) – Click to zoom in; Alt+click to zoom out. Or drag to zoom into a specific area.

Tip: Many tools have a keyboard shortcut (letter in brackets). Press the letter to switch to that tool. This speeds up your work.

Photoshop Tools panel: single or double column; click the double arrows at the top to switch tools.

Photoshop 2026 – Main Panels (Tabs) Explained

Panels appear on the right (or wherever you dock them). Each tab gives you different controls.

Layers Panel

The Layers panel lists every layer in your document from top to bottom (top of the list = front of the image). You can:

  • Click a layer to select it (your edits apply to that layer).
  • Drag layers to reorder them (change what is in front).
  • Show/hide a layer with the eye icon.
  • Lock a layer (transparency, pixels, or position) so you don’t change it by mistake.
  • Create a new layer, duplicate, delete, or group layers into folders.
  • Change opacity and blend mode for special effects.
  • Add layer masks (black hides, white shows) to hide parts without erasing.

Layers panel: list of layers, visibility (eye), lock, opacity, blend mode.

Color / Swatches Panel

Color – Lets you pick the foreground and background colour using a colour slider or by typing values (e.g. RGB, HEX). Swatches – Preset colours you can click to apply. You can save your own colours as swatches for reuse.

Properties Panel

Shows properties of the selected layer or adjustment. For a normal layer you might see document info; for an adjustment layer (e.g. Levels, Curves) you get the adjustment controls here. Very useful for non-destructive editing.

Adjustments Panel

Quick buttons to add adjustment layers: Brightness/Contrast, Levels, Curves, Exposure, Vibrance, Hue/Saturation, Color Balance, Black & White, and more. Adjustment layers change how the image looks without changing the original pixels; you can edit or remove them anytime.

Channels Panel

Shows colour channels (e.g. Red, Green, Blue in RGB mode) and any saved Alpha channels (selections saved as masks). Advanced users use channels for precise selections and colour work.

Paths Panel

Stores vector paths you draw with the Pen tool. You can turn a path into a selection, fill it, or stroke it. Paths don’t use pixels until you use them.

History Panel

Lists your recent steps. Click a step to undo back to that point. You can also take a snapshot to jump back to a saved state later.

Styles Panel

Preset layer styles (drop shadow, glow, bevel, etc.). Click one to apply it to the selected layer. You can also create and save your own styles.

You can open any panel from Window in the menu bar. If a panel is missing, go to Window and tick its name.

6. Types of Graphics

9 Raster and Vector Graphics

Learning focus: Difference between raster (pixel) and vector (math-based) graphics; when to use each.

Raster Graphics

Raster images are made of tiny coloured squares called pixels. Each pixel has one colour. Photos from cameras and phones are raster. File types: JPEG, PNG, BMP, TIFF. If you zoom in a lot, you see the squares and the image looks blocky.

Vector Graphics

Vector graphics are made of shapes described by maths (lines, curves, fills). They can be scaled to any size without losing sharpness. Logos and illustrations are often vector. File types: SVG, AI, EPS. Editing is done by moving points and curves.

7. Color Theory

10 Color Theory: Wheel, Warm & Cool, Tint & Shade, Primary & Secondary, RGB & CMYK

Learning focus: How colour works in design: the colour wheel, warm and cool colours, tint and shade, primary and secondary colours, and the difference between RGB (screen) and CMYK (print).

The Colour Wheel

The colour wheel is a circular diagram that shows how colours relate to each other. It helps you choose colours that look good together. The wheel is built from primary and secondary colours arranged in a circle.

  • Complementary colours – Opposite each other on the wheel (e.g. red and green, blue and orange). They create strong contrast and make each other stand out. Use for emphasis but avoid large areas of both.
  • Analogous colours – Next to each other on the wheel (e.g. blue, blue‑green, green). They feel harmonious and calm. Good for backgrounds and smooth visuals.
  • Triadic colours – Three colours evenly spaced (e.g. red, yellow, blue). They give a balanced but lively look.

Colour wheel: complementary colours are opposite; analogous are next to each other.

Primary and Secondary Colours

Primary colours are colours that cannot be made by mixing other colours. All other colours can be made from them.

  • In light (additive) – Red, Green, Blue (RGB). Mixing light: red + green = yellow; red + blue = magenta; green + blue = cyan; all three = white. Used for screens (monitors, phones, projectors).
  • In paint/print (subtractive) – Red, Yellow, Blue (traditional). Or in professional print: Cyan, Magenta, Yellow (CMY). Mixing pigments: cyan + magenta = blue; cyan + yellow = green; magenta + yellow = red. Used for printing and paint.

Secondary colours are made by mixing two primary colours:

  • Red + Yellow = Orange; Yellow + Blue = Green; Blue + Red = Violet/Purple (traditional model).
  • In RGB: Green + Blue = Cyan; Red + Blue = Magenta; Red + Green = Yellow.
Primary = cannot be mixed from others. Secondary = mix of two primaries. The colour wheel is usually drawn with primaries and secondaries in order: Red → Orange → Yellow → Green → Blue → Violet → back to Red.

Warm and Cool Colours

Colours are often grouped into warm and cool.

  • Warm colours – Red, orange, yellow. They remind us of sun, fire, heat. They feel energetic, urgent, or friendly. Use for call-to-action buttons, sale banners, food, excitement.
  • Cool colours – Green, blue, violet (purple). They remind us of sky, water, grass, shadow. They feel calm, professional, or cold. Use for trust, tech, health, relaxation.

In simple words: Warm = fire and sun (red, orange, yellow). Cool = water and sky (green, blue, purple). Choose warm when you want energy; choose cool when you want calm or trust.

Warm: red, orange, yellow. Cool: green, cyan, blue.

Tint, Shade and Tone

You can change a colour by adding white, black, or grey:

  • Tint – Colour + white. The colour becomes lighter and softer (e.g. red → pink). Tints feel light and gentle.
  • Shade – Colour + black. The colour becomes darker (e.g. red → dark red / maroon). Shades feel deeper and heavier.
  • Tone – Colour + grey (or both black and white). The colour becomes less vivid, more muted. Tones feel subtle and professional.

In simple words: Tint = add white (lighter). Shade = add black (darker). Tone = add grey (muted). Design software often lets you pick a colour then move a slider for lighter/darker (tint/shade).

Same colour (red): tint (light), pure, shade (dark).

RGB (Red, Green, Blue)

RGB is the colour model used for screens: monitors, TVs, phones, tablets, projectors. Light is added: Red + Green + Blue light in different amounts make all the colours you see. Each channel is usually a number from 0 to 255 (e.g. R=255, G=0, B=0 = red; R=0, G=0, B=0 = black; R=255, G=255, B=255 = white).

Use RGB when your design will be seen on screen (websites, apps, presentations, social media). In design software, choose “RGB” or “Screen” colour mode for digital work.

CMYK (Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Key/Black)

CMYK is the colour model used for printing. Ink is subtracted: cyan, magenta, yellow and black inks are printed on white paper. Each is usually a percentage (0% to 100%). K = “key” (black) – it gives sharp text and dark areas; CMY alone would make a muddy dark.

  • C – Cyan (blue‑green).
  • M – Magenta (pink‑red).
  • Y – Yellow.
  • K – Black (key).

Colours on screen (RGB) can look different when printed (CMYK) because screens emit light and print reflects light. Bright neon-like colours on screen may not print as bright.

Use CMYK when your design will be printed (brochures, posters, business cards, magazines). In design software, choose “CMYK” or “Print” colour mode and 300 DPI for good print quality.

8. Use of Graphic Design Software

11 Tools, Packaging, Import/Export and Slicing

Learning focus: Using graphic design tools; packaging; importing and exporting graphics/images; slicing.

Graphic Design Tools

Use the tools panel to: draw shapes (rectangles, circles, paths), add and format text, apply fills and strokes, align and group objects, and work with layers. Practice each tool on a simple document.

Packaging

Packaging (in some software) means saving the project and copying all linked images and fonts into one folder so you can move or send the project without broken links.

Import and Export

Import – bring images or graphics from files (e.g. JPEG, PNG, SVG) into your design. Export – save your design in a format for print (e.g. PDF, high-res PNG) or for web (e.g. PNG, JPEG, SVG). Choose the format that matches how the design will be used.

Slicing

Slicing means cutting a design into smaller pieces (slices), often for web use. Each slice can be exported as a separate image. Useful for buttons, banners or when you need only part of the design as an image.

Activity

In your design software, import an image, add a shape and text, then export the page as PDF and as PNG.

9. Ethical Uses of Graphics and Images

12 Copyright and Plagiarism

Learning focus: Using others’ work legally and fairly; copyright and plagiarism.

Copyright

Copyright is the law that gives the creator the right to control how their work (images, text, music, etc.) is used. You cannot use someone else’s image, logo or design for your project without permission or a licence, unless it is clearly marked as free to use (e.g. some Creative Commons or public domain).

Always check the licence of images and fonts. Use stock photo sites that offer free or licensed images, or create your own graphics.

Plagiarism

Plagiarism means presenting someone else’s work as your own. In design, copying another person’s layout, logo or image without credit or permission is plagiarism and can be illegal. Be original, or get permission and give credit where required.

In simple words: If you did not make it and you don’t have permission, don’t use it as if it were yours. When in doubt, create your own or use clearly licensed content.

10. Practical Work and Preparing for Printing

13 Image Editing Practical and Preparing for Print

Learning focus: Using image editing software; preparing text, images and graphics for different print types.

Image Editing Practical

Practice: opening an image, cropping and resizing, adjusting brightness/contrast and colour, using layers, making selections, and exporting in the right format and resolution for print or web.

Preparing for Printing

Different print methods need different file setup:

  • Offset printing – high volume (e.g. books, brochures). Use CMYK colour mode, 300 DPI, and supply PDF with bleed and crop marks if the printer asks.
  • Digital printing – smaller runs, fast. Often PDF or high-res JPEG/PNG; 300 DPI for quality.
  • Screen printing – used for T-shirts, posters. Often one or few colours; design may need to be simplified into separate colour layers.

Always confirm with the printer: file format, colour mode (RGB vs CMYK), resolution and page size.
